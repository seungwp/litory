## About the Author

About Yi Sang

Yi Sang (1910-1937) was one of Korea’s most innovative writers of modern literature, enough to deem him Korea’s finest modernist. He died at the early age of 27, but despite his short literary career, he produced surreal and highly experimental pieces that were avant-garde and far ahead of their time. He showed brilliant literary prowess not only in poetry and fiction, but also in essays, exploring the confusion and anxiety of those living under Japanese colonial rule, the psychology and despair of uprooted urban dwellers, and the alienation, disquiet, and terror experienced by intellectuals, more than perhaps any other writer in Korean history. He did not shy away from presenting decadent subject matter, and experimented ceaselessly with form, created self-deprecating characters with excessive self-consciousness, portrayed the delirium of sensation, and employed wit, paradox, montage, and other various techniques all to brilliant, enigmatic effect, to the extent that his works resist easy comprehension even to this day. These are the reasons why he was heralded as a “modern boy,” who sprung onto the literary scene during Korea’s dark colonial period.

Yi Sang’s fiction is largely autobiographical. From his sole novel December 12 all the way to his short story “Dying Words,” Yi Sang has used his own life as material. However through his unique method of processing those experiences, in other words, through his unique artistic method of handling language, his work continues to be cutting-edge even today.

“Dying Words” (1937) is a confessional story, narrated by a character named Yi Sang. By the narrator’s self-deprecating manner, the reader can tell that this character is a parody of the real Yi Sang. This story is one of three, along with “Wings” and “Child’s Bone,” that takes the relationship between man and woman as its subject. This particular story, which is about a man who loves an unfaithful woman, juxtaposes the past and present to build the narrator’s inner world. As suggested in the title, this work foretells Yi Sang’s own imminent death.

## Dying Words

“Having lost the coral between the cracks…” I have messed up the words of the poem already. I should be ashamed as I look up to the heavens, but its countenance, ever growing in human wisdom, is truer to life.

Even if I should die, I shall die clutching the coral whip. A phoenix shall come and perch on top of my tattered, poor scholar’s clothes, on top of my fading bones.

I am confessing my style’s thriftiness that is as stingy as my determination is desperate for my “Dying Words” to strike terror into all the gentlemen-scholars whose eyes are open under heaven.

A notorious soldier named so-and-so, who became a sudden hero from a single gunshot, did not blubber out some last words on the day his close to ninety years of life ended; in this way, he passed his death scene (very safely—whew!) with flying colors.

But our Leochika—Tolstoy’s nickname—packed his traveler’s bag and left home, which was fine, but he made a mess in his final five minutes. With some obnoxious last words, he destroyed the tower he had built for seventy years and made a permanent scratch on what was otherwise a good life.

I’m watching the lives of these imbecile saints as a sly observer, so that I will not commit the same offenses, being well aware of their mistakes.

I take the razor blade to the mirror. I make a mistake and scratch myself. I spill my brains.

But because I crash head-on with all the swarming “I”s and they do their best to make their own apologies, it is difficult to find the perpetrator.

And that is probably why the ignorant masses rest easy, saying that “monkeys are mimicking humans.” The truth is, humans are mimicking monkeys and in doing so, they do not understand the message of the past.

Aha, that’s it. It’s been a long time since people have shaken free from the impulsive habits of Adam and Eve. Stuck between reactions and reactions, when my finger becomes a prisoner of self-consciousness as brief as flint or a fiery flash of lightening, I rub the bridge of my nose that has become a bizarre rock neglected at the center of my meaningless days. Or since there is a window that allows for a very brief moment between the noble conversations that are laid out like iron chains, the instant the gleaming blade cuts swiftly through self-consciousness, I worry that there might be sleep in my eyes—my eyes which are my most valuable treasures and should be as clear as the clearest mirror—and take out a suitably wrinkled handkerchief to rub at them—

I cannot handle the overwhelming array of generalization that is reported back to me by the gentle laziness of my soul and body that follows (and comes back after seeing) every trivial plume of smoke from a furnace.

But I would like to boast of my most treasured coral whip.

“Trash” and “leaf.”

Sir, do you understand the mood of these lonely words?

I think on the day you were married in a Christian ceremony, you must have felt at the nave and aisle a similar depth of feeling as that given off by “trash” and “leaf.” Is it not so?

I’m trying to scatter ribbons like “trash” or “leaf”—the tedious ornaments planted pitifully on every part of my dying words—

Fortunately there is applause. The end.

* * *

“The extravagant girl,” “standing by a stream bank during the spring thaw,” “her lips turning pale like falling petals,” “wondering what moves under the thin ice,” “lowers her head as if to peek,” “just then comes a balmy wind full of spring air,” “skirts,” no, no, “to touch the very.” No, no, “the somewhat,” “sad-looking red hair,” enough. No more. But I should make an earnest effort to add another wretched phrase.

“Fluttering, fluttering.”

This should be good enough as a fully-equipped device. In order to clearly depict the famous coral whip that will decorate the opening of my dying words, I have prepared these undeservedly extravagant and formidable furnishings above.

However—

Perhaps this is too much. Since there is enlightenment under heaven, I worry that one may become hastily exposed if it’s not gilded enough. But—

Let’s just write it as it goes.

I am lying alone, dying in my filthy room where the autumn wind is quite desolate.

According to my father and mother, I am without a doubt “a beautiful boy of rosy cheeks,” aged 25 years and 11 months, who is as small as an animal that has shed its thinning fur in autumn. And yet I am definitely an old man. Every single day “life is short and art is long.” It’s an incredible life.

Every day I am fated. When I awake from the sleep I was sleeping—how long ago did I begin this sleep—a bone-painful life starts and even though I lie down and cover myself up with blankets, I witness my youth being squandered.

I dine impoverished of songs. I will face my death in twelve hours and I cannot avoid thinking about this and that, searching for the last words that might have gotten lost somewhere, and having found them, I pick out the few that seem sophisticated.

But in this lonely final year I cannot gain a single epigram and I die the horrible death of a criminal.

A single day of a life—

A life of a single day ended, and then ended, above all else (before anything), like this.

Here—watch this.

There is a feeling that putting on cosmetics like this is much too extravagant.

But there is also a feeling that such an investment is well spent in order to raise up a Hamlet (speaking lowly of himself in his last words) who has no equal in this era, in either style or presence.

I am autumn. The girl is the spring thaw.

When will these two meet and play house together?

Again that spring—

I was forced to face the pathetic, unlucky comings and goings of the sun and moon with a body dignified with frost and ill at ease with the futile world.

Beautiful prose, beautiful prose, a dim opened mouth! Beautiful prose.

Beautiful prose is a dangerous business that cannot be dealt with easily.

It is so easy for me to die in dimness, anesthetized like a butterfly that was initially heading for a mountainous forest but got stuck in a honey jar of emotions. I know I need to fix my style with care.

I don’t know what I was thinking about that morning, but while I was brushing my teeth, I became sick because of the last will and testament I was currently working on.

Nearly thirteen wills were nearing completion. But none of these could go a step further than being a mere imitation of the supreme and singular work that a certain “genius” left beside his pillow before killing himself. It was the source of my anxiety and the source of my anger and bitterness, for that was the limit of my talent. I did not forget to wrinkle my forehead to keep up the appearance of a most noble face, and while I was in ceaseless pain (I don’t waste a single minute and I don’t quit because of my lack of wisdom but keep squeezing it out), a letter came express for me. It was from the girl.

Dear sir! I saw you last night in my dream. You are truly affectionate in dreams. You dote on me like a child.

But under a cloudless sky, you are distant and you do not call on me.

If you would like to see the color of cowardice, please look into a mirror. The color of your face reflected in the mirror is the color of cowardice.

Your only pride seems to be that you never told your ex-wife to get out while you lived with her for three years! Is your kindness so pathetic?

I have made a clean break from R. And you do believe it has been five months since I have ended my relationship with S, don’t you? For five months, I have had nothing. I hope you will be able to acknowledge my fidelity.

I will give you something of mine that has never been tainted. It is a shame that the beauty of my white skin has had nothing to do for five months. My neck covered in soft peach fuzz, my cool, refreshing heat—they are waiting for you. Dear sir! Please call on me. The fact that you never ask me to return, is this your pathetic idea of an apology, borne out of the same theory from when you left?

Dear sir, I love “only” you. Please, please make me yours, your one and only. Make me “exclusively yours.”

You must think that I am naïve, but you are wrong.

I want to tell you something. You might think you’re safe and secure, but that safe place of yours will be struck with disaster with just one word from me. Also—

Damn it! It’s a disgusting life, so I want to act this way.

You must come to the bus station at Small East Gate on March 3rd, 2 o’clock in the afternoon or something bad will happen and you won’t be able to escape my punishment.

From Jeonghui

Now 19 years and 2 months old

To Mr. Yi Sang

Of course the whole thing is a lie. However, there is a sense of such precarious caution that it could blow up with one small touch, and this is especially true at the end of the letter—an exquisite piece of prose with a sudden turn the likes of which one might see in strong gales and lightening strikes.

I nearly faint with my tongue hanging out. I decide to be fooled for just a moment. I end up being fooled.

I, the one called Mr. Yi Sang, the one like a scarecrow, experienced my whole life the night before. After reaching a wrinkly old age I saw the morning (when it came) and oh! I try to sleep (a sleep), cheekily, in full view, as soon as possible, but I have the habit of brushing my teeth and then going back to sleep right away. And I’m going to do the same today.

People look at me, and because they think it so bizarre, they are fooled into thinking that I am someone who is burdened with an ambitious plan to move heaven and earth. That is the only method with which I could maintain this spiteful appearance. In other words, I take my naps for others to see.

But after receiving her letter, I flitter in happiness like a wren taking flight and decide to wash away my worries about my will and my plans to turn the world upside-down, and head to the barbershop at once. Like some rare hero, I sit in front of the dazzling mirror with my lips covered white in toothpaste, to have my look prepared before the curtains are raised for my death, so that I can enjoy the show at leisure.

First of all, my unkempt hair, infamously named a magpie’s nest, is cut and made into a flattop. My five strands of facial hair neatly die out. My ears are cleaned and my nostril hairs are trimmed. I even receive a massage. After washing my face with soap I glance into the mirror, but alas, not a single trace of class can be found, but what can one do with how one was born? Although I have no evidence, I want to insist that I am handsome, if only one could see my face as that of an elegant novice who could very well be a member of the Pre-Raphaelites.

Dear me! I realize that I, too, have a hat. I take it out and dust it off, it having been crushed somewhere all winter, and take it to a 15-minute drycleaners and make it whole again. When I wear those burgundy suspenders with white pants and a shirt, my attire seems quite original. It has no satin luster, but with a twilled old-fashioned overcoat, I daresay my appearance could be compared to that of such and such geniuses from antiquity to present day. I remember to allow myself have proper attire that is neither thick nor thin in order to insufficiently modify my insufficient appearance.

Left with no alternative—

In fact, today is March 3rd.

With dignity, I arrive at the promised location at the Small East Gate half an hour late. Jeonghui, like the Jeonghui she is, in a Jeonghui kind of way, had arrived half an hour early.

She is standing like a sorrowful Tsarist Russian postage stamp. This is perhaps because the icy wind, since the frozen ground is thawing right now, is cold enough to make her seem sad for sometime.

I walk toward her with heavy steps, thinking that in a situation like this, to have the eyes well up with tears is perhaps just the right kind of thinking outside the box.

We stroll daintily, as though we are a pair of swallows that have lighted on land for the first time. As we walk I am careful about every wrinkle forming on my overcoat, as well as any crookedness that stirs my attire. This is all because I want to gloriously flaunt my coincidental death, so I am vowing that I will not accidentally ruin my pose of treading on thin ice.

Then according to my first statement, what strange and heartbreaking epigram must I pull out, and it is because of this that I shouldn’t hesitate for a moment? But I can’t just rush along ahead, because I will then end up saying something stupid, like being as anxious as a Tsarist Russian postage stamp.

I blurt out, “Can ‘can it be’ kill a person?”

I blabber closely into Jeonghui’s ear, my voice deep and rumbling from my belly, as I fashion the words in clear pronunciation. I think this is quite successful for my very first vocalization in such a situation. Meaning, it is my specific intent to let you know that you could not have possibly dreamed that I would actually come.

I taste the slick pleasure that is like skillfully managing to buy only three jeon’s worth of bean sprouts for breakfast when they had refused to sell only three jeon’s worth. I feel as though I’ve been very good at not wasting one bit of my precious money.

But Jeonghui doesn’t respond to my greeting, which should be like a bolt of lightning falling from a clear blue sky. This is really bad.

It looks as though she will knock me down in one strike with a small, bold gesture, like children playing the game “to eat a pepper, buzz, buzz, to eat tobacco, buzz, buzz.”

If this is actually true!

The firm and unassailable manner of this new Jeonghui is not merely a show. I sense the small blade, which I have unsheathed to kill, pulverizing and falling to the ground like some pathetic, low creation.

I signal my final absolute despair by gesturing wildly with my hands and feet. Since things have come this far, it no longer matters whether my mannerism is right or wrong. Since there doesn’t seem to be any facial expression left for me to assume, I try to fix my complexion a little and say, “Jeonghui! I’ll be off now,” and I bid her a polite farewell and then…

I turn around and start walking home. My turbulent life, full of ups and downs like the waves, has been reduced to ashes with a single petty word. Thinking that I have died a cruel death, I try to at least make it seem that way by whirling around my overcoat a few times and moving my lips slowly, pretending as though I were walking in procession.

5 seconds—10 seconds—20 seconds—30 seconds—1 minute—

I make my best effort to not turn around. I try to look as though I have been defeated and have no selfish motive left. I try to look as though I have lost my heart.

I am actually a little dizzy. My weak heart is unable to keep up this sluggish gymnastics for much longer. How about some tombstone inscriptions? On a cloudless day in the evening hours of March 3, in the year Anno Domini 1937 according to the Gregorian calendar, the rare genius, Yi Sang, after living a life full of ups and downs like the waves, suddenly died, leaving behind his magnum opus titled “Dying Words.” He was 25 years and 11 months old. Oh, what a loss! What a gaping void he has left behind! The other Yi Sang remaining in existence wails to the nine heavens and erects a memorial stone on a cold mountain. Your lover Jeonghui has become a slave concubine to many men after your downfall and is actually enjoying a long life, so Yi Sang of the underground! I pray that you will shut your eyes.

The mess isn’t too bad, so let’s leave it as is and cover up some of the shabby flaws. They shall be filled in with the acrobatic feats above and I have decided to carefully consider half my life’s future lineup. The end.

All the epigrams in history and the ironclad rules of collapsing nations are nothing more than a smooth excuse to secretly bury my hypocrisy. In order to rationalize my death where my fate falls, I will slip to my death just as abruptly as though twisting my ankle and falling, since I don’t see a paint palette the way Corot saw it, or possess a memory of a maxim as tiny as a rat’s tail the way Tolstoy did.

“Love” comes looking for me, carrying holiness as one of its names. I reply to it by raking in, at a bargain price, the dying instructions that had been picked clean and discarded by those wily virtuous men, both young and old; I smelt them together and use them again.

Or this is what I thought, but I only punish myself.

After just a spoonful of cold rice and a sip of cold water, I have the skill and wisdom to point out the “bitter truth” that will sufficiently overpower an era.

The secret of my death throes, which has raised itself above the height of my identity, has been given away for just a few coins to the countrywoman who sells fairy finger mushrooms at the night market. This I have already quite easily mastered, as well as the way of humility that can dress up my etiquette. It is a strange thing to be observed with wide-open eyes. The end.

This subject that is as ragged as muddy feet is not something that I, who come from a family line of good standing, am in the position to choose. I need to be careful and carefully purposeful about even the way I drink a cup of tea in the tradition of the west.

Even when I whistle, I must follow the procedure that I have learned from the ancients, which was carefully selected in utmost secrecy. Without doing so, I won’t be able to whistle to my heart’s content in that hopeless twilight.

How about a lofty knowledge of animals?

Except for the deer and duck, both sweet and pitiful, other species of animals must be exempt from my animal kingdom. I am always so stubbornly ignorant about animals that are not for hunting use.

Also—

Then how about an arrogant rule of conduct toward landscapes?

Rather than asking what landscape, but speaking instead of the landscape’s origin, center, and focus, I stress outrageously that I should at least act like a “Young Master.” I must believe in this blind dogma with my eyes shut tight.

It is truly difficult to volunteer to be “stupid” and “confused.”

Look at this. This self-satisfied end-all talent of stupidity! This end-all talent of confused.

The white albatross is right for white sands, so do not go near the spring grass that is green.

Li Bai. The grand nobleman for all time. I must become like him. So I must make at least one self-possessed mistake in any of the five-character quatrain lines. In arrogant superstition, everyone pretends to be artless and goes along with being fooled, even when one with an ornate pedigree boldly makes an elegant and authoritative flaw in a line of an ancient poem.

Because I must wake up from every upturned lament with a naughty smile, as neatly as becoming completely fooled by a chemise that has been finely washed and finely ironed—

Today I have tried to trick the girl whom I am not certain is one of my kind and tripped over myself instead; I have revealed this worthless drop of stain on my long-cherished, elegant death, throwing my life away like a spoon, so how can I possibly face my original purpose? This is the handsome excuse that emerges toward my front court, which is in fact a palm shorter than the night of eternal day.

Sluggish, trustworthy statement!

I immediately grab hold of an unlikely point in space and buy a pack of cigarettes out of thin air (putting it in my pocket) and light one and return to following Jeonghui’s predictable footsteps.

I whistle as though I am overlooking a common daily thing, and as I get a hold of myself and take control of such annoying details, like the tempo of my heels hitting the pavement, I am able to walk shoulder to shoulder with Jeonghui in approximately three minutes. As if protesting that nothing can be done about my distress in this futile world, I let my sad eyes wander toward an uncertain spot on the new landscape beyond the Small East Gate, as if to let her see my dogged, annoying posture, which is one of my best acts that I am hastily trying to improve.

A wooded mountain before sundown.

The day grew dark. Dear me! I think it is better to say that it has not yet grown dark.

The day has not yet grown dark.

If so, it seems that the universe has sent me a golden opportunity before my very own belly, to bring out the furnishings I had prepared to arrange them one by one for housekeeping. Now—

A daughter who cannot hide her lowly “position” because she cannot overcome her origin. (The such and such that was said previously about the extravagant girl came from this stupid Yi Sang’s perverted interpretation. Take Maupassant’s “Ball of Fat” for example. The family prostituted the daughter who was less than fourteen years old. The second time, she herself volunteered before turning nineteen. Ah—the third time, the spring of the year she turned twenty-two, she sold food and drinks, pretending to be a married woman with a child, by braiding her hair according to custom with a scarlet ribbon and letting down her decorative hairpin, which was a part of her dowry.)

A lowly daughter of a certain family, as she stands by the stream bank during the spring thaw, her lips turning pale like falling petals, wondering what moves under the thin ice, lowers her head as if to peek, just then comes a balmy wind full of spring air, and skirts, no, to touch the very sad-looking, no the somewhat sad-looking red hair and—

To touch the somewhat sad-looking red hair, fluttering, fluttering—

It is as it is above. Before me is the ridiculous, greasy stage and I, as I am, like myself, resolve to remember the petty tone that is one’s family as though I am stepping over a quarry step, and say in a kind of lament, “Those who slice off Earth are real destroyers of nature” or something like, “It’s the ant’s house that is in good order.” Or like, “If it rained, ah—if it rained on all that’s under heaven,” or even “The plants and trees that grew last year could sprout again this year. What is it when that which returns does not return again?” or something along those lines.

I pick out the quietest ones that would seem quite sophisticated with some dressing up and make them look rather careless.

Could it be true? Or is it a lie? Jeonghui suddenly speaks. She says, “Spring has come like this.” She covers her upper teeth where she has a little gap she thinks is ugly, but at the same time she attempts to cover her pretty lower teeth, but she displays them unwittingly as though to say, “What can be done now, since they have been seen already?” and presents them on top of her impenetrable, ambiguous face.

Good, good, good, this is good enough.

I nod not my head but my attire and in my hurry, I end up putting my hand on Jeonghui’s shoulder.

She is silent for a moment, as though she thinks this a little bizarre.

But then, would you believe, she tells me in a whisper that she, too, can guess at what is going on, since she has learned about lineages or, to put it simply, about etiquette?

Gulp!

My worthless death that is being swallowed up like this, if mistakes are allowed like this, where is the original intention of the coral whip that I have defended by squandering my whole material life? My rage surges up, to the point that I feel as though I am about to swoon.

I seem to have spun many lies for a lonely night before my thirst for death could lead Jeonghui into a gloomy back room of Heungcheon Temple, the Temple of Heaven’s Beginning.

As I have planned in deep, fragrant secret, I now see that the various rare tools, which tried to disturb the eternal libertine Yi Sang’s petty literature slum, have suddenly started to become dull, What about society, what about morality, introspection, pursuit, exposure, what about punishment, what about excessive self-consciousness, all those sleek, extravagant billboards? Every one of them is ridiculous.

“Poisonous Flower.”

Sir, why don’t you keep this single, sufficient marionette-like phrase for the time being?

Squeezing into the stench coming off the dead city’s kin, who crawl beside the futile furnace called art, more so than beside a corpse, I—

Shred apart my girl’s underskirt, make rags out of her socks, destroying her dark hair and fair complexion, as though a ferocious bear had walked all over her face, steal all my relatives’ money, ruin a venerable inn, fool a cowardly moneylender, make the moneylender’s bill collector swoon, drive a wedge between the boss and the director and the in-laws and the husband and the father and the brother-in-law and the sister-in-law and the father again and the father’s daughter and his other daughter, mislead them and make them fight, spill ink and the chamber pot and some red bean soup on the new tatami floor of my rented room, make someone impotent—

Now do you somewhat understand that piercing sensation at the phrase “Poisonous Flower”?

Because of the crime of thinking that appearing humbly with a few lines of poetry and several pieces of fiction resembling improperly made rice cakes is worth something, and then hastily clapping one’s hands once or twice at being fooled, one is more humiliated than if his girl had committed adultery, and having done all this there is a cheap procedure of etiquette, which dictates that one must pretend as if nothing had happened, which is similar to whining about it—why don’t I just say that this was the deed (infection) of an evil spirit (the genius of the household)?

“Poisonous Flower.”

Of course, it is possible that on a familiar street at daybreak tomorrow, I may finally be reunited with a covert libertine who is my equal in every sense, but I do not easily give up my self-mortification of being lashed by wind and rain, even as I’m raising and lowering my shoulders excitedly like a shaman in manifestation.

Ah—for some reason my entire body is itchy. I must be dressed as a villain because of the many grudges that indifferent people hold. Secretly sick to my stomach, I wash my hands cleanly in a clean chamber pot and return to my seat and calmly repent—to put it simply I should lay out a petty count.

Etiquette? Lineage? Formality? Metamorphosis?

Having said that, it would be absolutely ludicrous if I took my hands off Jeonghui’s shoulders right now. It will ruin everything. I just need to be careful about the flush in my cheeks and keep saying, “Good, good, good,” and that is all I need to do. Acting as if I have understood everything and with my hands still on her shoulders, we walk shoulder and shoulder into the grounds of Heungcheon Temple. Once inside, we act as though we have suddenly lost our way and calmly climb up to the top of the mountain. At the top, I hesitate elaborately, as though I am completely knocked over this time with no chance to assume a pose. But in reality I am completely fine.

The sound of the wind chime is just right. In a situation like this a person with decent learning could—

Ah—why do I always step away from convention? Have I forgotten? About the noble learning and etiquette I gained by offering my expensive monthly salary?

In a situation like this, why can’t I present, fair and square, the stern, austere education that I received from a lieutenant colonel?

At this dejected, venerable temple, in this device without enlightenment, I must come to my senses. I must be honest in using my distinguished background. I must lower my head and smoke a cigarette and tolerably succeed in holding a solo performance of a hasty and clumsy pose that I hate worse than death, like a cow being dragged into an abattoir.

Then she says stop it. Stop your preposterous act. She says she is quite fed up with it.

Then what?

Then wouldn’t all my ceaseless efforts turn into bubbles one morning or night?

It means that a pitiful “stony woman’” named Jeonghui has somehow managed to detect at least this much of my wicked scheme.

I am immediately fatigued. My spirit goes slack and what lies before me begins to spin, making me dizzy. Thinking that I am going to faint, I try to focus all my strength on what I am wearing, but no! It looks as though it will be very difficult this time for my death to become restored.

Yi Sang! You are a cripple, who does not know how to manage the world. Are you so “deluded”? While you were crossing, you find yourself at the temple site, don’t you? Even so, why don’t you have a look at Brothers Karamazov or Klim Samgin?

No! Jeonghui! This is the most excellent and terrifying spell among all the superstition that I’ve intentionally created, a kind of con, a trick borne out of my need to stay alive so that you can as well.

Yi Sang! Stop! Why don’t you place just one foot, just one step into the doggish muddy field there as a test?

When I stagger within this funny story that is as smooth as a musical score, I feel near my eyes and eyelashes the presence of a spotless libertine equal to me. Anyone would scramble to get rid of my unkempt pose. At that moment I feel my descendants being miscarried under my roof, and I place before me this talisman of an extremely wicked and secret lineage of firstborns, which has appeared over ten thousand years, and I feel a bit of anxiety, as well as a bit of complacency, over this loss of soul that resembles my death.

I urge Jeonghui with a sad heart, as though searching for a humble plot of land to dig my grave, and come down from that hill. If I plagiarize that the wind chime I hear behind me provokes me to the highest degree, it will help tidy this touching landscape. Then the wind chime behind me seems to stir up my last sullenness to another level.

A wind chime that closely resembles a fine landscape is as dangerous as a piece of beautiful prose. If a wind chime that resembles a fine landscape is adapted and copied into beautiful prose, it is like a puddle where one might easily drown, so no one should go near it. Dostoyevsky and Gorky acted as though they had no habit of writing beautiful prose, and they did not “deal” with elegant or wasted landscapes; these sly adults looked as though they were about to write beautiful prose, as though fine landscapes were about to appear, but in the end they are like old snakes that do not completely show their tails, so their tricks are much more advanced, and so, their effects are absolute, and for thousands and tens of thousands of years, they can fool the masses who desire only meaningless comfort. But—

Why is it that when I look at the dignity of a piece of modern architecture that soars sleekly in the sky, I sense immediately its steel frames and iron bones, its cement and fine sand? This cry of fate that I cannot wash away, this Mongolian fleck, this loneliness, this desolate dignity, this isolation, this agony, like falling and still rising, falling and still rising, like a roly-poly toy, so that it does not matter whether one falls or rises, having not one thin wall to lean on.

I have seen the light today, so I avoid beautiful prose and hide away the view of a fine landscape, and quietly slip into the afterlife, uncovering fate’s sad veil I was born with, and die under a refined, persuasive shade that is lonely but warm.

This soft, incomprehensible death; I must be dying young. No, this is what it means to be oppressed and disappointed while still alive. It draws near and I cannot overcome it, this libertine of libertines within the curse of the blind crow, this drunkard of drunkards, this impenetrable gate’s ruin, it is a bleaching out of the vanity among the decorations that infiltrate and contaminate every nook and cranny of the Savior’s Last Supper. It is the bleaching out of an uncommon genius.

The immorality of having a husband. The immorality of not having a husband. Immorality is enjoyable. Sir, have you known the taste of acting boldly without being burdened by the price of alcohol? A little gap in the upper teeth and fine-looking lower teeth, take a look at this face that can act coy like a bronze mirror from the Han Dynasty with its flawed beauty. Her only source of pride is that she powdered her face with pollen that was hidden inside a plantain lily until she was seven years old and that she has never washed or powdered her face since. Jeonghui is cross-eyed. Nothing can defy this. Jeonghui is nearsighted by six degrees. This is an innate badge that nothing can defy. A stigmatism in the left eye and color blindness in the right eye, ah—what is this if not perfection?

I was fooled after being fooled. I was fooled again after being fooled again. Jeonghui’s family forced her into prostitution before the age of fourteen. That’s what I thought had happened. A single drop of tear—

But around the time her family forced her, it was already long after Jeonghui had volunteered to be prostituted. A scarlet ribbon always fluttered behind her back. Her family believed in this precious scarlet ribbon to ward off disaster from immorality—

But—

Immorality is like a noble person and is truly enjoyable. An adulterous virgin— she is an eternal jungle that cannot help but be enjoyed among all immorality.

So does Jeonghui stop there?

I introduce myself. I don’t want to part with Jeonghui, so I make a cruel introduction of myself.

I have never seen a rice stalk. I do not know how to ride a bike. I sometimes forget the year, month, and day I was born. With my own hands, I demolished an ancient castle of ten generations that came from a certain sky to be married, a 90-year-old grandmother acting as though she were a 28-year-old bride, and with my own hands, I have cut down the base of a walnut tree that has been green for a thousand years. The ginkgo tree, carrying within its marrow the embittered family, was chopped down and every four years, when it became spring, sprouts like poisonous seeds bloomed.

But I have endured it all. Once I seized a pomegranate tree and made my way out of the ruins.

Unripe, overripe, brain-numbing stench of rotting persimmons. A pale flower bloomed in the shade of a smiling, desiccated body that had no equal in its roughness and wildness at the crossroads of death and life.

I painted a watercolor before the age of fourteen. Watercolors and poverty. Observe how steam rises from the wrist that is as thin as a wooden chopstick in the coldest month of winter. Steaming wrists and parasitically seductive skin where fine peach fuzz grows from prostitution. Her crossed eyes and my uneven ones that show no whites. Cosmetics from the old days like bizarre magic from the plantain lily and the complete abolition of cosmetics, countered by my precarious nature that does not know how to ride a bike. The immorality of a scarlet ribbon and my helpless nakedness that neglects immorality.

Oh, Judge! Do I not have more shortcomings than Jeonghui?

So they’re about the same? I shall fight till the end.

A gloomy back room at the Heungcheon Temple, two cushions, one furnace. A table for food and alcohol—

The sum of dozens of challenges. Hitting and smashing to the left and right. I come in through Jeonghui’s loose and empty gateway with the strength of an old death. But the counter attacks from the lethal weapon are several times fiercer than when going in, making me inflict injury on myself.

Do I lose? Do I lose and give up?

I decide to introduce my last weapon to this battlefield. It is none other than drunken frenzy.

It is difficult to look after just one body. I feel as though I am about to throw up. I throw up. On Jeonghui’s skirt. On her stockings.

Still I am unsatisfied. I stand up and dance. I shove open the double windows at the back of the room and saying that I will jump out and kill myself, I shake the handrail with everything I’ve got, except for the last ounce of strength that I’m saving. Jeonghui grabs me, trying to stop me. She’s trying to stop me, but it looks like she’s not trying. I grab at her skirt. Something falls with a slap. It’s a letter. I pick it up. Jeonghui pretends not to notice.

Express delivery. (You do believe it has been five months since I have ended my relationship with S, don’t you—the letter is from that S.)

Jeonghui! I’m angry with myself! What happened last night at the Western Villa? I had no intention of doing that to you. I did not take you there so that I could demand that of you. Please forgive stupidity. But I will take your surprising calmness as a small comfort.

Of course, I will not forget your passionate words, urging me to waste not even a day in making you my one and only. But unlike what you may think, it is not so easy dealing with the ugly thing that is my “wife.”

I will wait for you today (March 3rd) at exactly 8 o’clock in the evening in front of Golden Residence, at the same place from before. I want to apologize for what happened yesterday and I want to walk through the pine forest with you, since it appears the moon will be bright. Let’s also talk about our futures as we stroll together.

Morning of March 3rd, S

It is an express letter she received after sending an express letter to me.

Everything has ended. Last night, Jeonghui—

With that face Jeonghui had sent an express letter to me—Mr. Yi Sang—and with the same face she had met me. It was a metamorphosis close to terror. In order to feel and enjoy this trembling ecstasy she had taken over the innocent Yi Sang. I have been fooled and fooled again and fooled again and again and fooled again and again and again.

Of course I end up fainting right there. I die. I wander the afterlife. In the underworld, the moon is bright. I shut my eyes again. From the sky, a voice asks, How old are you? I am 25 years and 11 months. An early death, I see. No, sir, it is an old death.

When I open my eyes Jeonghui is gone. Of course, it is past 8 o’clock. That’s where she has gone. Thus my death has ended, but my dying words have not ended. Why?

Because even now Jeonghui is sitting in a chair in some building, untying the string of her underwear, even now she has her head resting on a Western Villa cushion, untying the string of her underwear, even now she is lying on a coat laid down on the floor of a pine forest, safely untying the string of her underwear.

This is a disaster I cannot simply stand by and watch.

I grind my teeth.

I pass out easily.

I seethe.

But now I would like to step aside from the heavenly grudge. I am longing for the warm peace of my mind or some similar thing.

In other words, I am a corpse. I realize that a corpse has no right or ability to envy the lord of all creation.

Jeonghui or her uncomfortably warm breath may sometimes graze my tombstone. When that happens my corpse turns red like a carrot and sends out a wail that pierces the nine heavens.

Meanwhile Jeonghui will have washed and left her many blankets (even the one with my stain) out to dry under the brilliant sun. If only the sad memory of my life will fly away from my corpse to the limitless sky for the sake of my many-layered dowry—

I am thinking such pathetic thoughts right now. Then—

Mr. Yi Sang, now 26 years and 3 months old! Oh, you scarecrow!

You are an old man. You are a skeleton whose shoulders go above his ears. No, no.

You are your distant ancestor. The end.

November 20th, Tokyo
