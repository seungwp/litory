The Digital Library of Korean Classics is a project undertaken by Literature Translation Institute of Korea (LTI Korea) to digitalize selected translated titles of Korean classics published in the late nineteenth to the early twenty-first century.

LTI Korea is an affiliate of the Ministry of Culture, Sports, and Tourism of the Republic of Korea that seeks to promote Korean literature and culture around the world.

This e-book was made by scanning and converting the original book using OCR software. We have made every effort to ensure the book is free of any errors or omissions, but if you discover any, please email us so that we can improve the quality of the book.

## PREFACE

Not much of the literary heritage of Korea, a nation that takes pride in her extensive cultural history, has been introduced to the world readership. It is a pity indeed that the image Korea has built up in the contemporary world scene is mainly that of a materialistically oriented nation rather doing well in the arena of competition in economic prosperity. It is sad, too sad, to witness the ignorance in Korean cultural heritage, especially of literature, on the part of the world population, with the possible exception of those few in her neighboring countries.

Having had a glimpse of the literary scenes in the English-speaking nations, I have undertaken a self-imposed mission—helping the English-speaking people partake in the pleasure of reading Korean literature, in my translation. The present volume is an outcome of the struggle I have embraced in an attempt to fulfill my wish to introduce part of Korean classical literature to English readership.

Reading literary works in translation, however, is not the royal road in getting acquainted with a literary tradition alien to one’s own. In order to become fully immersed in the literary tradition nurtured by the culture unique to a nation, one has to learn and master the language that is its verbal medium. But, alas, it’s only an ideal, and is not always feasible. So long as the lines in translation echo the original in verse rhythm and meaning, however, one must feel satisfied, for then the requirement of approximation to the original may be considered to have been met. In presenting this slim volume, I hope this small wish of mine has been fulfilled.

This volume is not meant to be a textbook; but I certainly hope that those who wish to tackle the kasa poems in their original will find the recto-verso en face parallel texts helpful. This book can function as an introduction to Korean classical poetry and also as a tool for improving one’s reading comprehension of the poetic lines composed in Korean in foregone times. It is in this spirit that I have added Commentaries and Notes at the end of the book. A book of poetry with a considerable number of pages allocated for explanatory notes may look odd; but my intention is not to present this book with a scholarly façade but to provide the readers with an apparatus helpful for their understanding of the lines, both in the original and in my translation.

One of the problems I had to face was the Romanization of the proper nouns in Chinese appearing in the original, which were to appear both in my translations and in my notes. Professor Kim Hae-myung, who teaches classical Chinese literature at Yonsei University, kindly took the trouble of going through my typescript to regularize the spelling and correct the errors I initially made. I thank Mr. Stanley H. Barkan, poet and publisher, of Cross-Cultural Communications, for agreeing to have this volume published. The philosophy he harbors about presenting dual texts, the original and the translation, side by side, concurs with what I envisioned at the outset; and I am happy to have my book published at CCC. I also acknowledge the debt I owe to the Korean Culture and Arts Foundation and Korea Literature Translation Institute for the grant and assistance they have provided for publication of this work.

—Sungl-Il Lee

April 2007, Seoul

## INTRODUCTION

kasa

INTRODUCTION

Korea is a nation blessed with her own unique orthography for the phonetic representation of spoken language. Before hun-min-jŏng-ŭm, now called han-gŭl, the phonetic writing system invented by King Sejong the Great (1397-1450) and his court scholars, was propagated in the middle of the fifteenth century, Chinese ideography was the sole means for written communication. After the invention and propagation of hun-min-jŏng-ŭm, however, poetic composition was done both in classical Chinese and in the vernacular.

Composing in classical Chinese, of course, required the background of a comprehensive knowledge of Chinese classics and mastery of the firmly set rules in poetic composition established by the Tang masters. As Chinese is a tonal language, and since the prosodic rules in poetic composition were strictly based on the tonal quality of the sound of each character, learning how to compose in that verbal medium must have required strenuous exertion of intellectual vigor and artistic gift. The fact that many Chinese men of letters were amazed at the poems composed in Chinese by contemporary Koreans attests to the high level of literary accomplishment the latter had attained, despite the deep chasm of the linguistic barrier between Korean and Chinese.

Composing in the vernacular must have provided relief from the tension of composing lines in an alien tongue and in its ideography, for now it was simply a matter of recording what flowed in one’s native tongue in an easy-to-learn writing system fit for its phonetic representation. Throughout the Chosŏn dynasty period (1392-1910), however, composing in the vernacular, utilizing the home-grown orthography, hun-min-jŏng-ŭm (or han-gŭl), was often looked down upon as a non-scholarly activity—something that one could indulge in from time to time at leisure. The way Latin was the verbal medium in the world of serious learning and scholarship in Europe for such a long time in the past, mastery of classical Chinese remained the main criterion of scholarly achievement, and consequently, composing in the vernacular, by resorting to han-gŭl, a ‘non-orthodox’ writing system, was not deemed to deserve whole-hearted scholarly and artistic devotion of a person of learning. In spite of the not-so-high regard for composing poetic lines in the vernacular, however, men and women with solid background of scholarship in Chinese classics wrote also in their native tongue, and left numerous works that deserve critical attention as manifestations of their love for their mother-tongue as well as genuine expression of their literary gifts.

Poetic composition in the vernacular was done in two different ways: one type was called shijo, the other kasa. A typical shijo consisted of three lines, each line containing fourteen-to-sixteen syllables evenly divided into four units of breath, each unit constituting one beat: thus, a shijo was made up of twelve evenly distributed and rhythmically evolving units of three-to-five syllables. In this verse form, verbal economy was a must; and within the limit of three lines, each with a set number of syllables—fourteen to sixteen—a poet had to produce a piece satisfying in the delivery of a poetic message, while meeting the structural requirement for verbal symmetry of three lines, each with four beats.

Let us read one shijo composed by Sŏng Sam-mun (1418-1456), one of the court scholars who participated in the project of inventing the writing system, hun-min-jŏng-ŭm, under the direction of King Sejong the Great. When the boy-king Tanjong (1441-1457), grandson of King Sejong the Great, was deposed by his usurping uncle, Prince Su-yang, the second son of King Sejong the Great, those who were loyal to the deposed king, including Sŏng Sam-mun, plotted a coup d'état for his reinstatement. The conspiracy was divulged, and those who had been involved in the scheme were all executed. The following shijo is known to have been composed by Sŏng Sam-mun when he was invited to join the group of those who supported the usurping prince:

이 몸이 죽어가서 무엇이 될고하니

봉래산 제일봉에 낙락장송 되었다가

백설이 만건곤할제 독야청청 하리라

What would I like to become after my death? Having turned

Into a weather-beaten pine on the highest peak of Mount Pong-nae,

I alone shall remain green as ever when white snow covers all.

(My translation)

In its original text, the above shijo contains three four-beat lines, each of which containing fourteen-to-fifteen syllables, which are evenly divided into four units (or steps) of breath:

i-mom-I / chug-ŏ-ka-sŏ / mu-ŏ-shi / toel-ko-ha-ni // [3-4-3-4]

pong-nae-san / che-il-bong-e / nang-nak-chang-song / toe-ŏt-ta-ka // [3-4-4-4]

paek-sŏ-ri / man-kŏn-kon-hal-je / tog-ia-ch’ŏng-ch’ŏng / ha-ri-ra // [3-5-4-3]

kasa, on the other hand, is unique in the sense that it really doesn’t have any form. This type of poetic composition is not under any restrictions of prosodic scheme or number of lines. As a matter of fact, one who decided to compose a kasa could let the writing brush glide on sheet after sheet till a work finally reached its end. In some sense, kasa can be considered an equivalent in spirit to ‘blank verse’ in English poetry. The prosodic scheme of iambic pentameter in blank verse surely does not apply to kasa. But so long as the metric requirement of thirteen-to-sixteen syllables per line and the symmetrical balance of the on-verse and the off-verse, each containing sustained two-beat rhythm, were met, a kasa could continue till its composer stopped writing. ‘Formlessness,’ so to speak, was the main feature of this poetic subgenre. The four-beat rhythm inherent in all poetic lines composed in Korean may be considered the internal evidence that a kasa was meant to be a poem. But in the sense that a kasa is not bound to follow any prosodic rules and that it tends to retain the spirit of prose-like carefree-ness, we may consider kasa to be a unique form of Korean classical poetry in prose.

Let us take a look at the opening lines of Song of Indulgence in Spring (Sang-ch'un-gok) by Chŏng Kŭg-in (1401-1481):

홍진에 묻힌 분네 이내 생애 어떠한고

옛 사람 풍류를 미칠가 못 미칠가

천지간 남자 몸이 날만한 이 하건마는

산림에 묻혀 있어 지락을 모를것가

수간 모옥을 벽계수 앞에 두고

송죽 울울리에 풍월주인 되었세라

hong-jin-e / mu-ch’in-pun-ne / i-nae-saeng-ae/ ŏt-tŏ-han-ko // [3-4-4-4]

iet-sa-ram / p’ung-niu-rŭl/ mi-ch’il-ka / mon-mi-ch’il-ka // [3-3-3-4]

ch’ŏn-ji-kan / nam-ja-mom-I / nal-man-han-i/ ha-kŏn-ma-nŭn // [3-4-4-4]

sal-lim-e / mu-ch’iŏ-it-sŏ / chi-rag-ŭl / mo-rŭl-kŏt-ka // [3-4-3-4]

su-kan / mo-og-ŭl / piŏk-kie-su / ap-e-tu-go // [2-3-3-4]

song-chuk / ul-ul-li-e / p’ung-wŏl-chu-in / toe-ŏt-se-ra // [2-4-4-4]

You who live in the dusty world, judge how my life is—

Whether it’s worthy of the ancient sages who lived in grace.

Though I’m no wiser than many born between heaven and earth,

Should I, living in retreat, not know the consummate pleasure of life?

Having built a hut near a clear stream. I dwell among the thickly grown

Pines and bamboos, and have come to own the wind and the moon.

(My translation)

No manuscript or copy made from the woodcut-engraved mold bears any indications of pause for breathing, or of line division, which may suggest versification. The rhythm inherent in the original text, however, allows a reader to figure out where to give pause while reading: after each set of four beats, or four units of two-to-four syllables, and at each caesura between the on-verse and the off- verse, each with a set of two beats. A similar circumstance exists in editing the texts of Old English poetry. The extant manuscripts of Old English poetry do not carry any clue to versification or line division. The scribes wrote down words from the left end to the right end of any given line, leaving no space in the margin, sometimes even changing lines right in the middle of recording a proper noun. In appearance, the manuscript of an Old English poem does not look like poetry but prose. But the scholars, who are well versed in Old English prosody, could edit the texts in such a way that the printed pages clearly show versification, including the symmetry of the on-verse and the off-verse with a caesura in the middle of a line. It may sound like too much of a far-fetched comparison between editing the text of a kasa and editing the text of an Old English poem; but there is no denying the parallel situations of the two analogous cases.

As in the case of shijo, the ultimate purpose of composing a kasa was to have it sung, or recited attuned to musical notes. It was not meant to be read by eyesight, but to be heard while slow and long-winded recitation was going on. Both shijo and kasa were composed under the presupposition that they were going to be recited by a performing artist well trained in the musical use of his voice. The term, kasa (‘歌辭’), literally means ‘words for singing,’ or ‘words to be sung.’ However, no one nowadays will have the patience to sit through a long recitation of a kasa, made interminably long, with all the prolongation of the vowels and the musically-adapted cadences, as it used to be ‘sung’ in olden days. Nor is that sort of oral performance available any more. Only non-authorial manuscripts and copies made from woodcut-engraved molds are left for us to have access to the texts of the kasa poems.

Depending on its authorship, a kasa can be classified as ‘aristocratic,’ ‘non-aristocratic,’ ‘Buddhist,’ or ‘feminine.’ Most of the works contained in the present selection are, as a matter of fact, from the first category. Only one sample of each of the two categories, ‘feminine’ and ‘non-aristocratic,’ is featured: A Woman's Complaint (Kyu-wŏn-ka) and Spring Slumber (Ch'un-myŏn-gok). No Buddhist kasa is included in this volume. Although selection of the works for inclusion in this book may appear arbitrary, I have my own reasons for it:

Chŏng Kŭg-in’s Song of Indulgence in Spring (Sang-ch’un-gok), the first to appear in this book, is generally believed to be the very first kasa ever written—the prototype of all kasa poems. Two main pillars of this poetic subgenre throughout the Chosŏn dynasty period were Chŏng Ch’ŏl (1536-1593) and Pak In-ro (1561-1642). The former was a typical man of letters of the mid-Chosŏn dynasty—actively involved in the world of politics as a government official, while enjoying his own share of pleasure as a man of gusto and self-indulgence. The latter was not only a man of letters, but a man-at-arms, who accomplished many military feats during the Seven-year War (1592-1598) waged against the invading Japanese. I have included five works by each, as the central pieces to be introduced in this volume. (Hence the title of the book: The Brush and the Sword.) Besides the two works mentioned earlier—one ‘feminine’ and one ‘non-aristocratic’—I have included two more works that can be classified as ‘aristocratic,’ one containing the debate on fame-and-glory vs. transcendence-and-retreat (an interesting Korean counterpart of the Western literary convention of pastoralism), and an impressive landscape-drawing composed by a man supposedly blind. With Spring Slumber, whose author is unknown, as the last work to appear in this volume, the works are introduced in the chronological order of the years of birth of the poets.

In translating kasa into modern English verse, all along I have tried to make the rhythm in the original text reverberate in my English version. In any kasa a reader is bound to feel sustained musicality arising from the regular beat repeatedly heard in the lines. I have tried to transplant the rhythm into English verse; but, in many cases, producing an exact replica of the verbal resonance of the original in translation, I have found out, is impossible. Perhaps one should feel content by believing that, what really matters in translation is not the physical length of a line, or the number of syllables in it, but whether the rhythm, the beat, in the original can also be felt in translation.

Nowadays, an ordinary reader of kasa will feel uncomfortable about the overabundance of literary allusions to Chinese classics and historical events. Should all these obscure references be faithfully ‘translated,’ or be explicated in the guise of translation? As in all other cases, common sense is a clue to the right answer; and maintaining the golden mean is always the right choice. These Chinese words and phrases appearing in kasa had already been fully assimilated into the language of the literate people in the olden days; and incorporating them in composing kasa was necessary, not only for effectively conveying the poetic messages but also for sustaining the verse rhythm by utilizing the economy of verbal expression inherent in them. To strike a balance between remaining faithful to the original even in translation and producing a text that can function as living verse in the target language has always been a source of dilemma for all translators of poetry. Perhaps I should seek contentment in what I consider my small technical achievement— making the verses in my translation correspond to those in the original, line after line.

—Sung-Il Lee
